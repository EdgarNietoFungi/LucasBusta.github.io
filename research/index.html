Research

Figure 1: Overview of plant metabolism

Plants are often considered to be boring and are frequently taken for granted, however, they are absolutely critical to human life; they provide us with food, fuel, and medicine. Currently, more than 15,000 species of plants are currently used by humans for medicine, more than 10,000 for materials, and 5,000 for food. 1

As sessile organisms plants have developed many chemical methods of responding to external stresses that other organisms may respond to with movement. To enable these chemical responses, plants have a highly advanced metabolic networks that provide them with access to a massive diversity of chemical compounds, many of which are not found elsewhere. We have only begun to scratch the surface of the diversity and function of these compounds with our current research efforts.

To contribute to the advancement of our knowledge of plant metabolites and their uses, I research plant lipid compounds. In particular, I am interested in plant lipid diversity and the different means by which lipids are biosynthesized, and in the plant cuticle, a lipidic structure that protects plants from drying out.

The plant cuticle

Figure 3: The lotus effect in action

Plants create large surface areas to maximize their photosynthetic capacity but in doing so risk getting dried out by the sun. To slow water loss, plants seal their above ground surfaces with a coating called the cuticle. These coatings are often superhydrophobic, causing water droplets to bead and roll off the plant, cleaning the surface as they do so (Figure 2). Plant epidermal cells coat themselves in cuin, a polyester matrix that serves as a molecular scaffold, and cuticular waxes, a mixture of very-long-chain lipids that accumulates within and on top of the cutin to seal the surface (Figure 3).
