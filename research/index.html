Research

Many organisms use movement to survive harsh environments. Plants, however, are rooted in the groud and more often use chemistry to solve problems. Having done this for millions of years, plants have developed complex metabolic networks that generate a massive diversity of chemical compounds, many of which are only found in plants or even in particular plant lineages (Figure 1; green pathways / compounds). These chemicals are absolutely critical to human life; they provide us with food, fuel, and medicine. Currently, more than 15,000 species of plants are currently used by humans for medicine, more than 10,000 for materials, and 5,000 for food (SOTWP 2016). Still, we have only scratched the surface of the vast pool of plant molecules that can cure diseases, contribute to better nutrition, teach us how to make new or better synthetic materials, and provide us with the genetic tools to improve existing biotechnologies.
My research focuses on the chemistry that plants do using fatty acids as metabolic starting points or building blocks. This research has both a fundamental and applied components that are defined by the following questions:
Fundamental research questions

1. How does the chemistry of compounds derived from fatty acids define the properties of hydrophobic biocomposites?


2. What is the structural diversity of plant fatty acid natural products and what is the genetic basis for these diverse structures?

Applied research questions

1. Can sorghum grain surface lipids be used as a platform for engineering a domestic source of high-value lipid natural products?


2. Can we use large chemical and genomic datasets to rationally identify lineages of tractable medicinal plants?
